The Queensland water sector this week is dominated by the final consultation push for the **SEQ Agriculture Regional Water Assessment** (closing December 12), with the Department of Local Government, Water and Volunteers (DLGWV) actively soliciting farmer feedback on five core options, including increased use of recycled water and new allocations from Sunwater's Maroon Dam. This strategic planning is occurring against a backdrop of operational vigilance, with **Toowoomba Regional Council** issuing fresh updates on water quality and maintaining low-level restrictions as summer approaches.

Politically, the focus is shifting to the intersection of water and energy costs. The **Queensland Farmers' Federation (QFF)** has escalated its advocacy on affordable electricity for irrigators, following a recent delegation to St George and ahead of tomorrow's National Agriculture Day breakfast with **Premier David Crisafulli**. The government has responded with cost relief measures for irrigators and significant red tape reduction for waterway development, signaling a proactive approach to addressing industry concerns.

Meanwhile, the **Paradise Dam** replacement project continues to advance under the new government's "Early Works" mandates, with recent fact sheets reinforcing the necessity of a full rebuild due to long-term concrete strength issues. However, **CANEGROWERS** and other grower groups continue to raise questions about timelines and water security, highlighting ongoing uncertainty despite government assurances. In parallel, regional councils have revived discussions around the **Nathan Dam** project (now estimated at $3.4B+), reflecting persistent demand for major water storage infrastructure across the state.

## ðŸ§­ Ongoing Issues Summary (Last month)

**[Paradise Dam Rebuild & Early Works]**

- **Status:** **Active / Construction Preparation.**
- **Update:** Following the mid-2025 "green light" from the Crisafulli Government, Sunwater has released new fact sheets (Oct 2025) detailing the technical basis for the full rebuild (concrete swelling and carbonation issues).
- **Context:** Road upgrades ($11M package) leading to the dam site were marked as complete in late October, enabling heavy vehicle access for the main wall construction. The government continues to frame this as "fixing the previous administration's failure."
- **Opposition/Criticism:** While the rebuild is welcomed, **Bundaberg Fruit and Vegetable Growers**, **CANEGROWERS**, and local irrigators remain critical of the timeline and continue to seek answers about water security. Industry groups have been vocal about the need for transparent communication, with ongoing questions about operational impacts and water availability timelines. The "uncertainty" around water security persists until the new wall is fully operational (expected late 2020s).

**[Burdekin Falls Dam Raising]**

- **Status:** **Planning & Enabling Works.**
- **Update:** Work continues on the 2-metre raising project. Recent budgetary confirmations (State Budget 2024-25) allocated $540 million to the project.
- **Dissent:** Environmental groups remain opposed to the raising due to potential sediment impacts on the Great Barrier Reef.
- **Sunwater Role:** Sunwater is currently managing the Environmental Impact Statement (EIS) and conducting geotechnical works at the saddle dams.

**[Reef Protection Regulations]**

- **Status:** **Stakeholder Conflict.**
- **Update:** The **Bundaberg Ag-Food & Fibre Alliance (BAFFA)** has issued a submission (Aug/Sept 2025) heavily criticizing the statutory review of Reef protection standards.
- **Core Conflict:** BAFFA argues the current regulatory framework is "fundamentally unsuitable" for the region's diverse, multi-crop farming systems, creating administrative burdens that do not align with on-farm realities.

**[Nathan Dam Revival]**

- **Status:** **Revived Planning / Feasibility Assessment.**
- **Update:** Queensland councils have revived plans for the Nathan Dam project with updated cost estimates exceeding $3.4 billion (June 2025). The project has been discussed for decades as a major water storage solution for agricultural and urban use.
- **Context:** Previous proposals included alternative approaches such as two weirs worth $45 million (2020), but the full dam project remains the preferred option for many stakeholders seeking long-term water security.
- **Regional Support:** Multiple councils are actively promoting the project, viewing it as critical infrastructure for regional development and agricultural expansion.
